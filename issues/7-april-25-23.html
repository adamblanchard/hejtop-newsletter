Today's focus is on communication. It's one of those elusive skills, with lots of interpretations and applications. It's important in every role and every stage of career, but suddenly the impacts of it become glaringly obvious when you're in a position of leadership.

Read below for a couple of useful ways to think about communication, both from a process perspective and a cultural one.

Always feel like you're stuck in the middle when communicating as a leader?

Everyone asking you questions, wanting status updates, and needing to manage expectations both in and out of your team. It gets overwhelming fast, especially as the organisation grows and the number of relationships you need to maintain increases.

Help yourself by focussing on and strengthening this communication loop:

1. Get context into your team.
They need all the context they can get to make accurate decisions by themselves. If a decision feels off, don't overrule, share more context.

e.g. "This feature is important because of this user research and this regulatory deadline"

2. Get key info out of your team.
Transparent, accessible and proactive updates to management, stakeholders and other teams. It should be filtered to be actionable for the specific audience. Include reasoning behind any decisions!

e.g. "We plan to release next Monday. We deprioritised X because of Y. We'll ask for your feedback on Friday so we can plan our next iteration"

...and repeat.

Ever sense a negative atmosphere creeping in the team?

One of the most impactful changes is to encourage "assuming best intent".

This means assuming by default that people's actions, decisions and communications are meant to be helpful, and not to be a dick.

It's easy to assume the worst when you're stressed or don't know them well. Stop that negative spiral in it's tracks by pausing, imagining how the person could mean well, and squish the bad assumptions.

Talk about this idea with your team, lead by example, and coach others in situations where you sense negativity as a first response.

In the bigger cultural perspective of your team, you can give "assuming best intent" the best chance of success by ensuring space for everyone to unpack personal expectations and build empathy with each other.

Make sure you have these in place:

  • Time for collaboration, working together, without deadline pressures.
  • "Getting to know each other" activities like coffees, hobby chats and social slack channels.
  • A people-first conflict resolution process, with active listening and no-blame at it's core.
