There is no standard API for humans

That makes it hard as an engineering manager because:
1. Everyone is different and has ever changing needs.
2. Many new people challenges pop up on the weekly.
3. You have many responsibilities to balance at once.

While you can't run one approach for everyone, build up your own frameworks for tackling certain themes that leave plenty of space for the above needs.



Let's break these challenges down some more:

1. Everyone is different and has ever changing needs.
Beneath our job descriptions, we are all uniquely shaped human beings; Our own values, motivations, interests and personalities. And these aren't static either, they change as our lives change. Think about all the times you've needed something different from your employer. Your first job, your first management role, that really stressful week, that year you're really pushing your career, those few months you need some breathing space. As a manager, we have to make space for humans and not just workers.

2. Many new people challenges pop up on the weekly.
There are themes when it comes to management: feedback, performance, deliverables, personal & career development and so on. However, humans are complex and unique and every time you face a new people challenge it will feel new. Maybe you feel comfortable having salary conversations until that one new time where someone feels undervalued, is angry and hands their notice in on the spot - what do you do then?! These new situatations never stop, and you have to act on the spot.

3. You have many responsibilities to balance at once.
On top of the above, you have a lot of responsibilities as a manager. Delivery, technical, people and process. All have huge responsibility trees on their own, which pull you all over the place. To put people first is tough because of these fighting priorities. We've all experienced those people challenges which take days (or even weeks!) of your time. That's not a complaint nor a problem, they deserve your attention, but they can shake up your whole schedule with no notice.

So what can you do?
Get ahead of these challenges by building up your own People Management Playbook (see below for a template). Naturally, as you gain experience and learn new things, you'll develop a mental model for people management and a set of approaches for how you handle different situations. The trick is to make this an intentional process by writing it down, reflecting and proactively filling your gaps.

While you can't develop a system to answer everything (see: humans have no standard api), you can totally build a guiding framework for different challenges along with techniques and resources to deal with them.

Use whatever tool you use to store notes. Structure a new section called People Management Playbook like this:
1. Themes
2. Situations
3. Approaches, Tools & Resources

List all the different people themes you deal with (hiring, performance, promotions, growth, conflict, feedback, goal setting etc.).

Next, under each of those, list a bunch of common (and not so common!) situations that come up, both hypothetical and based on your actual experiences.

Finally, to support you in those situations, collect your approaches, techniques, templates, resources, "expert contacts" and anything else that helps you tackle them in practice.

Now you have a place to visit, quickly navigate, and find approaches and supportive resources when you face these situations in the future. Remember to reflect and update situations as you experience new ones to continue your learning, and capture all the useful resources you come across in your people first management journey.
