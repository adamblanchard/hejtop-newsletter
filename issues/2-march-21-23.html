Team members see 100% of the problems in the team.

The higher up you go, the more the percentage drops.

We’ve all had those experiences when we realise the managers above us lack context of our own problems. It’s the nature of hierarchies. Don’t make the same assumptions with your own team.

This blind spot is overcome with humility - realise your team understands their problems better than you do.

Ground yourself regularly with these tips:
1. Stop (always) solving people’s problems for them.
2. Listen to deeply understand their experiences.
3. Unpack together what they need from you, not what you think they need from you.
4. Enable problems to be solved and use your position to get blockers out the way.



The deep dive

The further away you are from where a problem is realised, the less you will understand it.

Let's look how this law behaves in other contexts: Have you ever been involved in a user interview for a product you're working on? There is nothing quite like the mind blowing realisations you get once you witness people really trying to use your product in real life. You see people use your features entirely differently. You see people struggle in places you assumed were totally usable. You see a totally real insight into people's real experiences, instead of your own idea of how it is.

This new perspective hits you right in the face, even before you've managed to explain your new feature to them to solve a problem they haven't even thought about yet.

The reason we do user interviews is to get close to where problems are actually realised. We get to witness what works and what goes wrong. We get to connect with real humans experiencing it.

We go into user interviews open minded, ready to learn, and with the mindset that they understand their problems better than we do. That's why we're there. This is what humility in problem solving looks like.

Back to engineering leadership: We must apply the same mindset. Our job is to serve the people in our teams, and to do that we must understand their problems deeply. This requires us to be able to put our "expert" hat aside to overcome these blindspots we have. By genuinely listening to our team, we can help solve their real problems.

A big challenge for developers is finding rewarding work to work on.

Rewarding work means different things to different people, it could be:
1. Specific domains
2. Learning opportunities
3. Different responsibilities
4. Leadership opportunities
5. Certain technologies and tools
6. Impact for particular user groups

However, make sure you have the foundations for rewarding work in place before piling new stuff on top.

Do that by:
1. Providing “big picture” meaning for the team - so people get why.
2. Prioritising & defining tasks clearly - so everyone understands what's involved.
3. Managing workload intensity for each person - so they have space to enjoy their work.

Sometimes the stuff you do around your team’s challenges is just as necessary as tackling it head on.



The deep dive

It's easy and understandable as a manager to jump to the practical solutions when we want to help someone find more reward in their work. Don't get me wrong, it's incredibly important to help people find those new, different and rewarding opportunities so they can work on stuff that they find fulfilling.

However, it's equally as important to make sure the foundations for rewarding work are in place. Here's why:

It makes it easier for people to find rewarding work. When people understand why the work is important, and exactly what is required, they can find meaning in it. Not everyone will like or connect with the meaning, some people might love the idea of refactoring the whole codebase to upgrade to the framework version, and some people definitely won't. But having these foundations in place makes sure that the people who will find reward in it definitely will. It might not be a "new" thing they are looking for after all.

A note on "managing workload intensity": This is not about micromanaging tasks. This is about making sure everyone on the team feels like they have a reasonable workload to suit them as an individual, since it differs between different people and over times. It's about understanding and supporting this deeply.

Are you giving your developers enough space to grow?

Insecurities create barriers for your team. Check in on these on the regular:

1. Fear
You're worried something is going to go wrong so you take more control to decrease your anxiety (or to protect your team from failure).

2. "Doing" reward
You're missing the feeling of "doing" tangible tasks (harder to find in management) so you start taking over your team's responsibilities to feel like you're making a bigger impact.

3. Self-projection
While you're trying to help people grow, you only have confidence in what worked for you, so you project your own experiences and values too strongly onto others, which stops them finding their own path.

These aren't easy to avoid.

Self-awareness is key. Notice when these start to creep into your behaviours and tweak them fast.



The deep dive

Let's break down how to check in on these. Self-awareness takes a lot of practice to build up. The best way to start is to first reflect on previous experiences, break down how it went down, and start to rewire how you react for future cases.

1. Fear
Review the last week and pick three decisions you made or significantly influenced in your team. For each, write 5 WHYs under each, asking yourself why you took that decision. Look out for any reasoning that pops up related to anxiety, reducing risk, protection from failure and so on. Reflect on how you act if that fear wasn't a reality. How did the outcomes differ? Would you take a different approach?

2. "Doing" reward
This is a complex one. Take a moment to unpack what's motivating you here. The first step is to slow down and give yourself space to rewire. Engineering work is very dopamine heavy, and has many short feedback loops. Management is a totally different ballgame - nudging ideas, creating clarity, meetings, discussing, mentoring. They all lead to great outcomes but are way fluffier in the short term.

Try writing a personal win list at the end of each day. Write down all the "less tangible" things you've achieved today, and why they were valuable. Tie these to your team and company goals to understand how you're contributing to the long term outcomes.

If that's not the problem, and you genuinely struggle with delegation, check out the "Six Ts" framework to get you started.

3. Self-projection
The first step is to unpack your potential projections. Write down your career journey, key decision points, your motivations and strategies that you found worked. Make sure you capture those that you often find yourself advocating for others to try.

Next, go through these key decision points and write down 5 other possible motivations or strategies that could have lead to that change. e.g. if you previously wanted a promotion primarily for a higher salary, list other reasons that people might want a promotion that have nothing to do with salary.

Now you're prepared with better understanding your potential projections - watch out for when you're starting to push them onto others who are asking for advice! If you feel you are, take a moment to explore a few other potential motivations and options that they could take, like the ones you brainstormed earlier.
